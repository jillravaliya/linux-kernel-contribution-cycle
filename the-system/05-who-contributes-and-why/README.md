# Who Contributes and Why

---

> The Linux kernel is built by thousands of people. Some are paid full-time to work on it. Some do it on weekends for free. Some work for companies you have never heard of. Some work for the largest technology companies in the world. Why does Intel care about the kernel? Why does Microsoft — yes, Microsoft — contribute code to Linux? Why does Google fund kernel security work? And what about the person doing it alone, unpaid, at midnight?

**You're about to find out — who is actually building this thing, what they get out of it, and why the funding model behind the kernel is one of the most interesting things about it.**

## The Numbers — Who Contributes What

The Linux Foundation publishes a report every few years tracking who contributes to the kernel and how much. The numbers are striking.

```
Contributions by employer (approximate, recent cycles):

Company              % of commits
------------------------------------
Intel                ~10-13%
Unknown/None         ~8-10%  (independent/unknown)
Red Hat              ~7-9%
Google               ~5-7%
Samsung              ~4-6%
SUSE                 ~3-5%
IBM                  ~3-4%
Linaro               ~3-4%
ARM                  ~2-3%
Meta                 ~1-2%
Microsoft            ~1-2%
Oracle               ~1-2%
Huawei               ~2-4%
Others               remaining %
```

A few things stand out immediately:

First — no single company dominates. Intel is consistently the largest single corporate contributor but still represents only around 10% of commits. This is deliberate. No single company controls the kernel.

Second — "Unknown/None" is always near the top. These are independent contributors — people working without corporate backing. They are a significant force in the project.

Third — Microsoft contributes to Linux. This surprises people who remember the 2000s when Microsoft called Linux a "cancer." The business reality changed completely. More on this below.

### Contribution Types Matter

Raw commit count understates some contributors and overstates others. Different types of work have different impact.

```
Types of contribution and what they represent:

High commit count, lower individual impact:
└── Cleanup patches, coding style fixes
└── Documentation updates
└── Small driver fixes
└── Often done by newer contributors or automated tools

Lower commit count, higher individual impact:
└── Core subsystem architecture work
└── Security hardening
└── Performance improvements
└── New hardware support for major platforms

The kernel bot (automated fixes):
└── Some cleanup commits are generated by scripts
└── coccinelle semantic patches can touch hundreds of files
└── One person writes the script, hundreds of commits result
```

This is why the Linux Foundation report also tracks lines changed, review activity, and patch acceptance — not just raw commit count. A maintainer who reviews 500 patches and accepts 200 of them has enormous impact that never shows up in the commit count.

## Companies That Pay for Kernel Work

---

### Intel

Intel is consistently the largest single corporate contributor to the Linux kernel. The reasons are deeply practical.

```
Why Intel contributes so heavily:

Hardware support:
├── Intel makes CPUs, GPUs, NICs, SSDs, WiFi chips
├── Every product needs a Linux driver to sell
│   └── Server market runs ~97% Linux
│   └── No Linux driver = cannot sell to servers
│   └── Investing in upstream drivers = product works
│       on every distro, every kernel version, forever
│
Performance:
├── Intel's CPUs need to perform well on Linux
├── The scheduler, memory management, and power
│   management all affect Intel CPU performance
├── Intel engineers work on:
│   └── CPU frequency scaling (intel_pstate driver)
│   └── P-state and C-state power management
│   └── Hardware performance counters (perf)
│   └── Intel-specific CPU vulnerabilities (Spectre,
│       Meltdown — Intel engineers wrote the mitigations)

Security:
└── Intel Platform Security Division contributes
    security hardening, SGX support, TDX (Trust
    Domain Extensions) for confidential computing
```

Intel also funds the **0-day testing robot** — an automated system that builds every patch posted to the mailing list against hundreds of kernel configurations and reports build failures back to the author. This is one of the most valuable infrastructure contributions to the entire project. The robot catches thousands of build errors per year before patches reach maintainers.

### Red Hat / IBM

Red Hat was acquired by IBM in 2019. Together they represent one of the most consistent long-term investors in the Linux kernel. Red Hat engineers have been kernel maintainers for decades.

```
Why Red Hat / IBM contributes:

Business model:
├── Red Hat's entire business is selling enterprise Linux
│   (RHEL — Red Hat Enterprise Linux)
├── The better Linux is, the better their product is
├── They are literally selling Linux — of course they
│   invest in making it better

What they focus on:
├── Enterprise reliability and stability
│   └── Features large enterprises need:
│       high availability, live migration, huge systems
├── Virtualization (KVM)
│   └── Red Hat is the primary maintainer of KVM
│   └── The hypervisor that powers most of the cloud
├── Storage (Ceph integration, filesystem reliability)
├── Networking performance (for telecoms and cloud)
├── SystemTap and kernel debugging tools
└── Security (SELinux was developed by NSA, refined
    and maintained largely by Red Hat engineers)

Notable Red Hat kernel contributors:
├── Ingo Molnar — x86, scheduler, perf, locking
├── Peter Zijlstra — scheduler, locking
├── Paolo Bonzini — KVM
└── Many others across every major subsystem
```

Red Hat engineers hold maintainer positions across many critical subsystems. When Red Hat engineers say a change should not land, it often does not land.

### Google

Google's relationship with the Linux kernel is driven by two things: Android and cloud infrastructure.

```
Why Google contributes:

Android:
├── Android runs on the Linux kernel
├── ~3 billion Android devices in active use
├── Google maintains the Android kernel tree
│   └── Forks of mainline with Android-specific patches
│   └── Goal: get Android patches upstream into mainline
│       so they do not have to maintain a permanent fork
│
Cloud infrastructure:
├── Google's entire cloud runs on Linux
├── Performance improvements in the kernel directly
│   improve Google's infrastructure efficiency
├── At Google's scale, 1% scheduler improvement =
│   millions of dollars in compute savings per year

What Google works on:
├── BPF (Berkeley Packet Filter)
│   └── Google engineers are among the primary BPF developers
│   └── BPF is used heavily for networking, security,
│       observability in cloud environments
├── Kernel security hardening
│   └── Kees Cook (Google) maintains kernel hardening
│   └── KSPP (Kernel Self Protection Project)
│   └── CFI, stack canaries, ASLR improvements
├── io_uring — high performance async I/O
├── Memory management improvements
│   └── DAMON — data access monitoring
│   └── userfaultfd improvements
└── Scheduler improvements for cloud workloads

Google Summer of Code:
└── Google funds GSoC which brings hundreds of
    new contributors to open source every year
    including many who start with kernel work
```

### Samsung

Samsung's contribution is driven almost entirely by their hardware — they make ARM-based SoCs (System on Chip) for phones, televisions, and embedded devices.

```
Why Samsung contributes:

Hardware portfolio:
├── Exynos SoC series (phones, tablets)
├── Display controllers
├── Camera ISPs (Image Signal Processors)
├── Storage controllers (UFS, eMMC)
└── All of this needs Linux drivers

What they work on:
├── ARM architecture support
│   └── Device tree bindings for Samsung hardware
│   └── Clock and power domain drivers
├── DRM/KMS display subsystem
│   └── Samsung engineers contribute heavily to
│       the Direct Rendering Manager
├── V4L2 (Video for Linux)
│   └── Camera framework used by Android cameras
├── Thermal management
│   └── Phone thermal throttling to prevent overheating
└── Power management
    └── Battery and charging drivers

Samsung's ARM contributions:
└── Samsung engineers have been ARM subsystem
    contributors for over a decade
└── Marek Szyprowski — DMA mapping, ARM
└── Krzysztof Kozlowski — ARM64, device tree
```

### Microsoft

This one surprises people. Microsoft contributing to Linux was unthinkable in 2005. In 2024 it is completely normal.

```
The Microsoft story — how it changed:

2001: Steve Ballmer calls Linux "a cancer"
2007: Microsoft buys Hyper-V, needs Linux guests to work
2009: Microsoft submits first Linux kernel patches
      (Hyper-V drivers — Linux running in their hypervisor)
2011: Microsoft becomes a top 20 Linux contributor
2016: Microsoft releases WSL (Windows Subsystem for Linux)
      The kernel team now runs Linux inside Windows
2019: Microsoft joins the Linux Foundation as Platinum member
2021: Microsoft contributes to NTFS3 driver
      (proper NTFS support in Linux, written by Paragon)
2024: Regular contributor across multiple subsystems

Why Microsoft contributes now:
├── Azure cloud runs mostly Linux VMs
│   └── Hyper-V must work perfectly with Linux
│   └── Linux performance in Azure directly affects revenue
├── WSL — Windows Subsystem for Linux
│   └── Millions of developers use WSL
│   └── They need a good Linux kernel experience on Windows
├── Microsoft services run on Linux
│   └── GitHub, Teams, Azure services — all Linux
└── Developer relations
    └── Being a good Linux citizen matters for
        attracting developers to Azure and Microsoft tools

What Microsoft works on:
├── Hyper-V drivers (their original reason for contributing)
├── Azure-specific optimisations
├── WSL kernel patches
├── Security features (they have strong security teams)
└── NTFS filesystem driver
```

### Linaro

Linaro is less well-known than the others but extremely important for the ARM ecosystem.

```
What Linaro is:

Linaro is a non-profit engineering organisation
founded in 2010, funded by ARM, Samsung, Qualcomm,
Google, and others.

Its entire purpose: improve Linux on ARM.

Why it exists:
├── Before Linaro, ARM Linux was fragmented
│   └── Each SoC vendor had their own kernel fork
│   └── Dozens of incompatible ARM kernel trees
│   └── Nothing could be upstreamed cleanly
│
├── Every Android phone ran a massively forked kernel
│   └── Qualcomm had their fork
│   └── Samsung had their fork
│   └── MediaTek had their fork
│   └── None of them talked to each other
│
└── Linaro's mission: consolidate this into mainline

What Linaro engineers do:
├── ARM architecture consolidation
│   └── Merged dozens of duplicate platform codes
│   └── Created the ARM multi-platform kernel
├── Power management on ARM (cpuidle, cpufreq)
├── Toolchain improvements (GCC, LLVM for ARM)
├── Kernel testing infrastructure
└── Device tree standardisation

Impact:
└── Modern Android phones run kernels that are much
    closer to mainline than they were in 2010
└── This is largely Linaro's work
```

Meta's contribution is driven by the scale of their infrastructure.

```
Why Meta contributes:

Scale:
├── Meta runs some of the largest Linux deployments
│   on the planet
├── Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp — all Linux
├── At their scale, kernel improvements have
│   enormous economic value

What they focus on:
├── BPF — Meta is one of the biggest BPF users

│       network observability, performance monitoring
├── Cgroup improvements
│   └── Resource isolation for their workloads

heavily to the BPF tooling ecosystem, not just the
kernel BPF subsystem itself.
```

## Why Companies Fund Kernel Work — The Business Logic

The common thread across all these companies is simple: **they depend on Linux, so they invest in Linux**.

```
The business logic of kernel investment:

Alternative 1: Do not contribute upstream
├── Maintain a private kernel fork
├── Apply patches from mainline manually
├── Every kernel update requires manual rebase work
├── Your drivers break on every new kernel version
├── Your patches grow further from mainline over time
│   (called "fork drift")
└── Cost: enormous engineering overhead forever

Alternative 2: Contribute upstream
├── Your code is in mainline
├── Every kernel update keeps your code working
│   (other developers fix conflicts for you)
├── The community tests your code on hardware you
│   do not have
├── Bugs get fixed by contributors worldwide
└── Cost: engineer time to get patches accepted
         (once, not forever)

Most large companies eventually learn:
└── The cost of NOT contributing is higher than
    the cost of contributing
```

This is why companies fund kernel work. It is not charity. It is engineering economics. Maintaining a private fork of a 36-million-line codebase that changes 70 times per day costs more than having engineers upstream your changes.

## Independent Contributors

Despite all the corporate investment, a significant portion of kernel contributions come from individuals working without corporate backing — the "Unknown/None" category in the statistics.

```
Who independent contributors are:

Students and recent graduates:
└── Learning systems programming
└── Building a portfolio for job applications
└── Kernel contribution is one of the most impressive
    things you can put on a CV for systems roles

Systems programmers working on related tools:
└── Someone writing a userspace tool that needs
    a kernel feature finds a bug and fixes it
└── Distro packagers who find kernel issues while
    packaging software

Hobbyists with specific hardware:
└── "I have this obscure device and the driver is broken"
└── They fix it, upstream it, move on
└── Sometimes become long-term contributors

Former employees:
└── Left the company but still contribute to projects
    they care about
└── Often become key community members because they
    have no corporate agenda
```

### How Independent Contributors Survive

The honest answer is: most do it alongside a regular job. But there are paths for those who want to do it full-time.

```
Funding paths for independent kernel contributors:

Linux Foundation fellowships:
└── The LF funds a small number of developers
    to work on critical infrastructure full-time
└── Greg Kroah-Hartman was funded this way for years
└── Extremely competitive, extremely impactful work

Consulting:
└── Companies pay independent kernel developers
    for specific driver work or subsystem expertise
└── "I need this hardware to work on Linux, can you
    write the driver?" — common contract model
└── Maintainer expertise commands high rates

Patron/sponsorship:
└── GitHub Sponsors, Patreon, Open Collective
└── Works for maintainers with large enough communities
└── CoreJS maintainer attempted this with mixed results
└── More viable for tooling than kernel work

Getting hired:
└── The most common path
└── Contribute independently, build reputation,
    get hired by a company that funds kernel work
└── Your kernel git log is your portfolio
```

## Mentorship and Entry Points

The kernel community has recognised that new contributors face a steep learning curve and has created structured programs to help.

---

### Google Summer of Code (GSoC)

GSoC is a program where Google pays students a stipend to work on open source projects over a summer. The Linux kernel participates every year.

```
How GSoC works for kernel contributors:

Timeline (typical):
├── February: organisations (including Linux kernel) apply
├── March: student applications open
├── April: students selected, projects assigned
├── May-August: coding period (12 or 22 weeks)
└── August-September: evaluations, stipend paid

What students work on:
├── Usually mentored by a subsystem maintainer
├── Work on a specific project with defined goals
├── Real patches go to the real mailing list
└── Successful students often continue contributing

Stipend (2024 approximate):
├── Large project (22 weeks): $3000-6600 USD
└── Medium project (12 weeks): $1500-3300 USD
    Varies by country cost-of-living adjustment

How to apply:
└── kernelnewbies.org lists kernel GSoC projects
└── Reach out to potential mentors before applications open
└── Having some prior kernel contribution helps a lot
```

---

### Linux Kernel Mentorship Program

The Linux Foundation runs a mentorship program year-round, separate from GSoC.

```
Linux Kernel Mentorship Program:

Runs: Spring, Summer, Fall cohorts (3 per year)
Duration: 12 weeks per cohort
Stipend: ~$500/month (varies)

What it offers:
├── One-on-one mentorship from experienced developers
├── Structured introduction to kernel contribution
├── Specific project to work on
└── Direct path to becoming a contributor

Focus areas vary by cohort:
├── Kernel testing and CI
├── Security hardening
├── Device driver development
└── Specific subsystem work

Apply at: mentorship.lfx.linuxfoundation.org
```

---

### Outreachy

Outreachy specifically supports people who face systemic under-representation in technology — women, people of colour, and others. The Linux kernel participates.

```
Outreachy:

Runs: Two cohorts per year (May-August, Dec-March)
Duration: 3 months
Stipend: $7000 USD
Eligibility: People underrepresented in tech
             18+ years old
             Not currently a student (in some cohorts)

What makes Outreachy different:
├── Paid contribution period — not volunteer work
├── Dedicated mentor for the entire period
├── Community of other Outreachy participants
└── Many participants go on to full-time kernel careers

Apply at: outreachy.org
```

## The Full Picture

The Linux kernel is funded by a coalition of companies with competing commercial interests who all share one thing: they need Linux to be excellent.

```
Why the funding model works:

No single company controls it:
└── Intel cannot force a change that harms AMD
└── Google cannot force a change that harms Microsoft
└── The technical community makes technical decisions

Companies fund what benefits them:
└── Intel funds CPU performance work
└── Samsung funds ARM driver work
└── Red Hat funds enterprise reliability work
└── The sum of all these investments benefits everyone

Independent contributors keep it honest:
└── No corporate agenda
└── Will push back on changes that benefit one company
    at the expense of the project
└── Often become maintainers precisely because
    they are trusted by all sides

The result:
└── 36 million lines of code
└── Running on everything from a Raspberry Pi
    to the world's fastest supercomputers
└── Maintained by competing companies who
    cooperate because the alternative costs more
```

This is the most important thing to understand about the kernel community: the cooperation is not altruistic. It is economically rational. And that makes it more stable than altruism, because the incentive never goes away.
